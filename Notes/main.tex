\begin{abstract}
Smartphones operating under sustained computational loads, such as navigation applications in high ambient temperatures, 
are susceptible to overheating. This report evaluates the feasibility of using a passive pin-fin heat sink attached to the 
rear surface of a smartphone to reduce operating temperature. Classical steady-state fin theory is used to model heat dissipation 
as a function of fin material, fin geometry, and array configuration. Parametric studies are conducted to identify an optimal 
design and to assess the realism and limitations of the modeling assumptions.
\end{abstract}

% -----------------------------
\section{Introduction}

Modern smartphones generate significant heat during extended use due to high processor utilization and limited 
internal heat spreading. Excessive temperature rise can degrade device performance through thermal throttling, 
reduce battery life, and cause user discomfort. Because smartphones are constrained by size, power consumption, 
and noise, passive cooling solutions are particularly attractive.

This study analyzes a pin-fin heat sink mounted to the back surface of a smartphone and evaluates how fin length, 
material thermal conductivity, and fin array configuration influence the phone surface temperature under steady-state conditions. 
The objective is to determine whether a compact pin-fin heat sink can meaningfully reduce operating temperature while remaining 
realistic for consumer electronics applications.

The analysis is performed under the following assumptions: steady-state heat transfer, uniform base temperature 
across the phone surface, negligible thermal contact resistance between the phone and fin base, adiabatic fin tips, 
constant convection coefficient over all exposed surfaces, and negligible radiation heat transfer.

% -----------------------------
\section{Methods}

The smartphone is modeled as a uniform heat-generating surface of area $A_b$ dissipating a total heat rate $Q$ to an ambient environment at temperature $T_\infty$. 
A rectangular array of cylindrical pin fins is attached to the phone surface. Heat transfer occurs by convection from both the exposed base area and the fin surfaces. 
The phone casing and fin bases are assumed to be at the same uniform temperature $T_b$.

For cylindrical fins with adiabatic tips, a corrected fin length is used to account for end effects:
\begin{equation}
L_c = L + \frac{D}{4} \qquad \text{(Table 3.5)}
\end{equation}

The fin parameter is defined as
\begin{equation}
m = \sqrt{\frac{4h}{kD}} \qquad \text{(Table 3.5, footnote b)}
\end{equation}

The fin efficiency is
\begin{equation}
\eta_f = \frac{\tanh(m L_c)}{m L_c} \qquad \text{(Eq. 3.100)}
\end{equation}

The adiabatic tip assumption is appropriate because the fin tip area is small relative to the lateral surface area for slender pin fins, 
making tip convection negligible. Applying an energy balance to the phone surface yields the steady-state base temperature:
\begin{equation}
T_b = T_\infty + \frac{Q}{h A_{b,f} + N \eta_f h A_f}
\qquad \text{(rearranged from Eq. 3.105)}
\end{equation}

Parametric studies were performed using Python to evaluate the influence of fin length, material thermal conductivity, and fin array configuration on phone surface temperature.

High-conductivity materials produce substantially lower base temperatures than low-conductivity materials. 
Increasing fin length reduces phone temperature initially; however, diminishing returns are observed as fin efficiency decreases. 
Fin array configuration also influences performance, as increasing fin count initially increases the surface area available to convection, 
but after the optimal array, an increased fin count reduces exposed base area available for convection. Physically, too many fins can choke airflow, reducing effective convection.

\section{Discussion and Conclusions}

Material thermal conductivity is the dominant factor affecting fin performance. 
Aluminum 2024-T6 ($k \approx \SI{177}{W/m\cdot K}$) provides an order-of-magnitude improvement over stainless steel ($k \approx \SI{15}{W/m\cdot K}$), 
resulting in significantly lower temperature rise for the same geometry. Nonmetallic materials are therefore unsuitable for compact heat sink applications.

The convection coefficient used in this analysis ($h = \SI{50}{W/m^2\cdot K}$) corresponds to mild forced convection, 
such as airflow within a vehicle cabin. Under purely natural convection conditions ($h \sim \SI{5}-\SI{10}{W/m^2\cdot K}$), 
the predicted temperature reductions would be substantially smaller, indicating that airflow plays a critical role in real-world performance. 
The assumed heat dissipation rate of $Q = \SI{10}{W}$ represents a conservative upper bound for sustained smartphone operation.

Several model limitations should be noted. Radiation heat transfer is neglected and may contribute modestly at elevated temperatures. 
The steady-state assumption ignores transient warm-up behavior that may be relevant during short-duration use. 
Despite these limitations, the analysis demonstrates that a compact aluminum pin-fin heat sink can meaningfully reduce smartphone surface temperature 
when moderate forced convection is present. An array of 20 aluminum fins with a diameter of 3 mm provides a favorable balance between thermal performance and geometric practicality.

\section*{Appendix: Python Code}

\begin{lstlisting}
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Enable interactive plotting
plt.ion()

# Given constants
Q_total = 10.0          # W
T_inf = 20.0            # C
h = 50.0                # W/m^2*K
A_base = 0.06 * 0.124   # m^2

fin_lengths = np.linspace(0.001, 0.1, 100)

# Material properties
materials = {
    "Aluminum 2024-T6": 177,
    "AISI 302 Stainless Steel": 15.1,
    "White Pine Wood": 0.11,
    "Clay": 1.3
}

# Fin arrays
fin_arrays = {
    "10 fins, 5mm dia": (10, 0.005),
    "20 fins, 3mm dia": (20, 0.003),
    "30 fins, 1mm dia": (30, 0.001)
}

def phone_temp(Q, k, L, h, N, D, A_base, T_inf):
    Lc = L + D/4
    m = np.sqrt(4*h/(k*D))
    eta = np.tanh(m*Lc)/(m*Lc)
    A_fin = np.pi * D * Lc
    A_base_free = max(A_base - N*np.pi*(D/2)**2, 0.0)
    T_base = T_inf + Q/(h*A_base_free + N*eta*h*A_fin)
    return T_base, eta

# 1) Phone temp vs fin length (materials)
array_choice = "20 fins, 3mm dia"
N_fins, D = fin_arrays[array_choice]

plt.figure()
for material, k in materials.items():
    temps = []
    for L in fin_lengths:
        T_base, _ = phone_temp(Q_total, k, L, h, N_fins, D, A_base, T_inf)
        temps.append(T_base)
    plt.plot(fin_lengths*1000, temps, label=material)
plt.xlabel("Fin Length (mm)")
plt.ylabel("Phone Temperature ($^\circ$ C)")
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True)
plt.savefig("figures/materials_vs_length.png", dpi=300)

# 2) Phone temp vs fin arrays
material_choice = "Aluminum 2024-T6"
k = materials[material_choice]

plt.figure()
for array_name, (N_fins, D) in fin_arrays.items():
    temps = []
    for L in fin_lengths:
        T_base, _ = phone_temp(Q_total, k, L, h, N_fins, D, A_base, T_inf)
        temps.append(T_base)
    plt.plot(fin_lengths*1000, temps, label=array_name)
plt.xlabel("Fin Length (mm)")
plt.ylabel("Phone Temperature ($^\circ$ C)")
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True)
plt.savefig("figures/length_vs_phone_temp_arrays.png", dpi=300)

# 3) Optimal design
N_opt, D_opt = fin_arrays["20 fins, 3mm dia"]
k_opt = materials["Aluminum 2024-T6"]

temps_opt = []
eff_opt = []

for L in fin_lengths:
    T_base, eta = phone_temp(Q_total, k_opt, L, h, N_opt, D_opt, A_base, T_inf)
    temps_opt.append(T_base)
    eff_opt.append(eta)

plt.figure()
plt.plot(fin_lengths*1000, temps_opt)
plt.xlabel("Fin Length (mm)")
plt.ylabel("Phone Temperature ($^\circ$ C)")
plt.grid(True)
plt.savefig("figures/temps_vs_length_optimal.png", dpi=300)

# 4) Fin efficiency
plt.figure()
plt.plot(fin_lengths*1000, eff_opt)
plt.xlabel("Fin Length (mm)")
plt.ylabel("Fin Efficiency")
plt.grid(True)
plt.savefig("figures/efficiency_vs_length.png", dpi=300)
